brain.py is the personality file — this is where most of the fun lives. The 220 and 450 are the reflex distances (panic threshold and caution threshold). The 0.7 / 0.35 / -0.4 / 0.55 are cruise, creep, reverse, and pivot speeds. t + 0.6 is how long it backs up; random.uniform(0.5, 1.4) is the turn-length dice. Every one of these survives to the physical robot, so tuning them now is real work, not play — try a timid robot (creep at 0.2, panic at 350) versus a reckless one (cruise 1.0, panic at 120) and watch the character change.
sim.py is the world and body file — the walls list (add interior segments and build furniture: a couch is just four segments), start pose x, y, theta, TRACK for chassis width, dt, and the loop count. Adding a second wall segment jutting into the room is the best next experiment, because corners are where cheap avoidance logic gets embarrassed — see if you can build a wall pocket that traps it.
sensor.py — hands off, deliberately. It's certified by its four tests, and it models physics, not choices; there's nothing to tune. If you ever do edit it, the deal is: rerun python sensor.py before trusting anything downstream.
